Venting Smoke ‚Äî
Fireground Operations
Ventilation is not just opening a window. It is a planned, purposeful, officer-supervised operation. Done wrong, it spreads fire and kills firefighters.
Source: V. Dunn, Deputy Chief (Ret.) ‚Äî FDNY ¬∑ FDTN Vol. 30, Issue 1

"Ventilation before the line is ready can spread fire or burn searching firefighters."

V. Dunn, Deputy Chief (Ret.) ‚Äî FDNY

Four Purposes of Venting

  • To save lives
  • To assist the hoseline advance
  • To prevent or reduce effects of an explosion
  • To limit fire spread

Critical Timing Rule

  • Venting MUST be timed with the hoseline advance
  • Venting before the line is ready spreads fire or burns searching firefighters
  • The officer must give the order to vent ‚Äî no freelancing

Key Points by Situation

Multi-Story Residential: Vent the roof skylight, scuttle, or bulkhead door at the highest point over the interior stairway. Smoke rises, accumulates at the top floor, and asphyxiates sleeping occupants. Venting over the stairway removes the threat and saves lives.

Venting for Extinguishment: Vent the opposite side of the building from the attack. Hose streams are effective at 30‚Äì50 feet ‚Äî dense smoke and heat push crews back past that range.

Explosion Prevention: In strip malls and commercial buildings, early removal of a roof skylight releases expanding gases safely before a smoke or gas explosion occurs.

Concealed Roof Space: The most serious and frequent structural fire spread problem. Fire enters below the roof deck and spreads horizontally across multiple occupancies. A vent cut directly over the main fire area reverses the mushrooming effect.

üí° Drill Idea

Using a pre-plan from your first-due area, identify all vent points on the building. Walk through: Where is the concealed roof space? When does the vent crew act relative to the attack crew? Who gives the order?

Fire Suppression ¬∑ Hoseline Operations
Fire Attack Basics
Every suppression operation comes down to hose placement, water application, and coordinated movement. These basics don't get old ‚Äî they save lives every time they're done right.
Source: FDTN Vol. 30, Issue 1 ¬∑ For: Sergeants & Engineers

"The first line goes to the seat of the fire. The second line protects the first crew and the primary means of egress."

FDTN Vol. 30, Issue 1

Hoseline Selection

1¬æ" line (150‚Äì200 gpm): Standard residential attack line. Maneuverable in tight hallways and stairwells. Requires two-person team minimum.

2¬Ω" line (250+ gpm): Commercial, large-volume, or defensive operations. High flow for large compartments or exterior exposure protection.

Rule: Match the line to the fire load ‚Äî never underflow a large compartment fire. An undersized line gives the fire the advantage.

Line Placement Priority

Placement Rules

  • First line: seat of the fire
  • Second line: protects egress & backs first crew
  • Third line: secondary egress or exposure
  • Never advance past the fire ‚Äî cut off retreat
  • Keep the line between victims and fire

Nozzle Techniques

Straight Stream

  • Penetrates deep into fire compartment
  • Best for reaching seat of fire through doorway
  • Minimal steam conversion ‚Äî push fire forward if misused

Fog / Narrow Fog

  • Maximum steam conversion and cooling
  • Use in confined space with ventilation
  • Can push fire if used in wrong direction

Combination

  • Rotate between straight and wide fog
  • Good for moving through compartments
  • Crew must communicate during transitions

Advancing the Line

Stairways: Flake the hose on each landing before advancing. Never let the hose kink at the door ‚Äî the nozzleman needs slack. Assign one member solely to hose management on multi-story operations.

Hallways: Stay low, move fast. The officer calls the nozzle open before the fire room ‚Äî brief burst to cool the overhead, then advance through the door for final attack.

Backup crew: The backup firefighter keeps tension on the hose, watches the nozzleman's back, and calls a retreat if conditions deteriorate.

üí° Drill Idea

Set up a dry hose advancement drill in the station or parking lot. Practice: pulling and flaking on stairs, advancing through a doorway with a charged 1¬æ" line, and calling "water on" / "water off" communications between nozzle and pump operator. Time each iteration.

Excellence
Setting expectations is the foundation of a high-performing shift. Officers who establish clear standards ‚Äî and hold them consistently ‚Äî build the crews that perform when it matters most.
Source: FDTN Vol. 30, Issue 1 ¬∑ Officer-Level Discussion

"You cannot hold your crew to a standard you haven't clearly defined. Say it, write it, enforce it."

FDTN Vol. 30, Issue 1

The Officer's Standard

Excellence on the fireground doesn't happen by accident ‚Äî it's built during every shift through consistent expectations, regular feedback, and deliberate training. The officer who shows up and just "lets things run" is setting a standard too: mediocrity.

High-performing companies share one trait: their officers define what right looks like, model it personally, and correct deviations immediately and without hesitation.

What Officers Must Define

  • Apparatus check standards and timing
  • Turnout gear donning expectations
  • Radio discipline on the fireground
  • Accountability procedures
  • Post-incident debriefing format
  • Physical fitness expectations for shift

The Feedback Loop

Real-time correction: Address deviations immediately ‚Äî not days later. Waiting erodes the standard. A brief, direct correction on the spot is far more effective than a counseling session a week after the fact.

After-action review: Every incident ‚Äî even minor calls ‚Äî deserves a brief debriefing. What did we do well? What would we change? Even a 5-minute tailgate debrief builds a culture of continuous improvement.

Recognition: Catch your crew doing things right and call it out. Specific, genuine recognition reinforces the behavior you want repeated. "Good job today" is less powerful than "The way you managed that hose on the stairs was exactly right."

Signs of a High-Standard Company

  • Equipment is always ready ‚Äî no surprises on scene
  • Crew can articulate their assignment before arrival
  • Radio traffic is clear, concise, and professional
  • Post-incident debriefs happen without being prompted
  • New members are mentored, not just observed

Officer Discussion Prompts

  • What are the top 3 standards on our shift right now?
  • Where are we falling short ‚Äî and why?
  • What did our last incident teach us?
  • How do we onboard a new member to our standard?
  • What does excellence look like at 0300?
üí° Officer Activity

Gather your shift officers. Each officer writes down the top 3 standards they hold their crew to. Compare lists ‚Äî where do they align? Where are there gaps? Use this to build a written set of shift expectations that every crew member can articulate.

Suggested Shift Training Activities

Pick one or more per shift ‚Äî coordinate with your company officer before initiating.

01
Ventilation
Pre-Plan Vent Walk
Pull a pre-plan for a first-due target hazard. Walk the building and identify every vent point: roof skylights, scuttles, HVAC, concealed spaces. Discuss who vents, when, and on whose order. Debrief as a company after.
02
Fire Attack
Hose Deployment Drill
Pull and stretch your attack line in the parking lot or station. Practice: proper flaking on stairs, advancing a charged 1¬æ" line through a door, and calling water on/off with the pump operator. Time each evolution and compare runs.
03
Suppression
Nozzle Technique Review
Set up a water flow demonstration with a charged line. Practice transitioning between straight stream, narrow fog, and wide fog. Discuss when each pattern is appropriate. Review fire behavior during each technique.
04
Officer Development
Standards & Expectations Meeting
Officers: gather your crew and conduct a standards discussion. Write down your top 3 company expectations. Share them. Compare with other officers on shift. Create a one-page written standard that every crew member signs off on.
05
Accountability
Tabletop Scenario
Run a kitchen table scenario involving a two-story residential fire. Address: Who gives the vent order? What size line do you pull? Where does the second line go? What happens if conditions deteriorate? Debrief thoroughly ‚Äî no wrong answers.
06
Leadership
After-Action Practice
After any training evolution or call, conduct a structured 5-minute debrief: What was the plan? What did we do well? What would we change? Who improves from this? Practice makes the debrief part of your culture ‚Äî not an afterthought.
